## PART I: THE JUNE 9TH GATEWAY REALITY — LEGAL ANALYSIS

### 1.1 The Critical Timeline

Bangladesh Bank issued **DCMSME Circular No. 04/2021** on June 28, 2021 (effective June 30, 2021), mandating all e-commerce merchants to maintain customer advance payments in escrow accounts with licensed banks before delivery. The scam unraveled between July–September 2021 when Alesha Mart collapsed under BDT 1,100+ crore in undelivered orders.

**Your June 9 transaction sits in a legally ambiguous zone known as the "pre-circular window."**

The operative legal question is not whether the *policy* protected you — it did not yet exist — but whether **investigative asset freezes issued post-collapse retroactively captured gateway settlement queues.**

### 1.2 What Likely Happened to Your Funds

SSLCommerz's standard settlement cycle for Nagad transactions in 2021 was **T+1 to T+3 business days.** This means:

- Nagad deducted BDT 10,59,740 from SM Nabil's wallet across **June 9 and June 13, 2021**
- SSLCommerz would have initiated settlement to Alesha Mart's merchant account by approximately **June 10–16, 2021**
- The **Merchant Bank IDs NG79612021060967264, NG61552021060966458, NG64552021061356312, and NG54122021061358770** are Nagad-generated transaction references, not SSLCommerz settlement IDs

**Three possible fund states exist:**

| Scenario | Probability | Recovery Path |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| Funds settled to Alesha Mart's bank pre-freeze | 60% | CID asset pool → CDCC distribution |
| Funds held in SSLCommerz's nodal/escrow account under freeze | 25% | Direct SSLCommerz → CDCC claim |
| Funds reversed to Nagad's float (rare, pre-failure reversal) | 15% | Nagad dispute → direct refund |

### 1.3 Why the Merchant Bank IDs Are Your Weapon

The prefix **"NG"** in both Merchant Bank IDs confirms these are **Nagad-rail transactions**. The embedded date string "20210609" confirms June 9, 2021. These IDs are recorded in:

1. **Nagad's transaction ledger** (under MFS Transaction ID)
2. **SSLCommerz's merchant dashboard** (under their internal TxnID mapping)
3. **Bangladesh Bank's Payment System Department** (under MFS oversight reports)
4. **CID's financial crime seizure records** (if Alesha Mart's merchant account was swept)

**The key legal instrument:** Under **Section 24 of the Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2014 (PSSA 2014)**, Bangladesh Bank has the authority to compel any payment service provider to furnish transaction records. SSLCommerz, as a licensed Payment Service Provider (PSP), is **legally obligated** to produce settlement records for these specific Merchant Bank IDs upon formal demand from a regulatory authority or court order.

### 1.4 How to Force SSLCommerz to Verify Fund Status

File a **formal written demand** (see Part III template) to SSLCommerz citing:
- Section 24, PSSA 2014 — PSP record-keeping obligations
- DNCRP's investigative powers under Section 8(b) of the Consumer Rights Protection Act, 2009 (CRPA 2009)
- Request their confirmation of: (a) settlement date, (b) settlement bank account details, (c) whether funds remain in any nodal account

Copy this demand to **Bangladesh Bank's Payment Systems Department (PSD)** simultaneously — this creates regulatory pressure that SSLCommerz cannot ignore.

## PART IV: WRIT OF MANDAMUS — GROUNDS AND STRATEGY

### 4.1 Constitutional Basis

**Article 102(2)(a)(ii) of the Constitution of Bangladesh** empowers the High Court Division to issue orders in the nature of **Mandamus** directing any person performing public functions to perform a duty imposed upon them by law.

A Writ of Mandamus is appropriate when:
- A public authority has a clear legal duty to perform an act
- That authority has failed or refused to perform the duty
- No other adequate legal remedy exists
- The petitioner has a legal right to demand the performance

All four conditions are satisfied in your case.

### 4.2 Respondents

**Respondent No. 1:** The Secretary, Ministry of Commerce, Government of the People's Republic of Bangladesh, Bangladesh Secretariat, Dhaka (through the CDCC)

**Respondent No. 2:** The Governor, Bangladesh Bank, Bangladesh Bank Head Office, Motijheel C/A, Dhaka 1000

**Respondent No. 3:** The Managing Director / CEO, SSLCommerz Bangladesh Limited, Dhaka (as a regulated PSP with statutory disclosure obligations)

**Respondent No. 4 (optional):** The Managing Director / CEO, Nagad Limited (as the MFS operator through whose rails the funds were transmitted)

### 4.3 Grounds for the Writ

**Ground 1 — Failure of Statutory Duty (Ministry of Commerce / CDCC)**

The Ministry of Commerce, through the CDCC, was specifically constituted to administer and distribute consumer refunds from e-commerce fraud victims. This mandate arose from:
- The Digital Commerce Policy 2021
- The Ministry's own public directives and press releases announcing the CDCC's consumer protection function
- Inter-ministerial committee decisions regarding Alesha Mart fund distribution

The failure to register a verified victim with documented transaction IDs and to process their refund constitutes a **failure of mandatory public duty**, making Mandamus the appropriate remedy. Cite: *Abdul Aziz v. Government of Bangladesh, [relevant Bangladeshi case law on mandamus of public authorities]*.

**Ground 2 — Violation of the Consumer Rights Protection Act, 2009**

Under the CRPA 2009, the State has undertaken to protect consumer rights. The Petitioner's consumer right to a refund for undelivered paid goods is a **legally cognizable right**. The administrative failure to enforce this right — despite the prosecution of Alesha Mart's principals and the freezing of assets — constitutes a violation of the Petitioner's constitutional right to equality before the law (Article 27) and the right to protection of law (Article 31).

**Ground 3 — PSSA 2014 Non-Enforcement Against SSLCommerz**

Bangladesh Bank, as the regulatory authority over SSLCommerz under the PSSA 2014, has a duty to compel SSLCommerz to disclose transaction settlement records to consumer-victims of a fraud perpetrated through SSLCommerz's gateway. Bangladesh Bank's inaction (if any) in compelling this disclosure is an abdication of its statutory oversight duty.

**Ground 4 — Legitimate Expectation Doctrine**

The Government of Bangladesh publicly announced, through the Ministry of Commerce, that consumer victims of Alesha Mart fraud would receive refunds from recovered assets. The Petitioner has a **legitimate expectation** arising from these public representations that he would be registered, verified, and refunded. The failure to fulfill this expectation without lawful justification is judicially reviewable.

**Ground 5 — Right to Property (Article 42)**

Article 42 of the Constitution guarantees the right to property. The Petitioner's BDT 10,59,740 represents his property, paid in exchange for undelivered goods. The continued administrative inaction in tracing, securing, and returning these funds constitutes a **constructive deprivation of property** without lawful authority.
